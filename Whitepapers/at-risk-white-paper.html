White Paper — GovCon Program Execution

At-Risk Work & LNTP:
Execution Before the Contract

How defense and government contractors authorize, budget, execute, and integrate pre-award work—without blowing up their cost baseline, compliance posture, or revenue recognition.

Revelation Technologies LLC revtech.consulting April 2026

Executive Summary

Every GovCon PM has felt the squeeze: the customer needs work to start now, but the contract isn't signed yet. Sometimes you get a Limited Notice to Proceed. Sometimes you just get a handshake and a prayer. Either way, the work happens before the money is officially authorized—and the financial, compliance, and operational implications are significant. This paper walks through the end-to-end lifecycle of pre-award work execution: how to authorize it, how to budget it, how to run it, and—critically—how to fold it into the definitized contract without creating an accounting nightmare. Whether the at-risk spend eventually becomes billable or gets absorbed as cost of performance, you need a clean plan from day one.

Section 01

Understanding the Two Mechanisms

Pre-award work in government contracting falls into two distinct categories. They share a common problem—work happening before a definitized contract—but carry different risk profiles, authorization levels, and financial treatment.

⚠️

At-Risk Work

Work performed by the contractor at their own financial risk, typically before contract award, with no formal government authorization. The contractor bears 100% of the cost exposure. It's a strategic bet: you start early to maintain schedule, protect customer relationships, or preserve competitive position—knowing the spend may never be recoverable.

📋

Limited Notice to Proceed (LNTP)

A written authorization from the government directing the contractor to begin a defined scope of work prior to contract definitization. The LNTP establishes a cost ceiling, a scope boundary, and—critically—a government obligation to reimburse allowable costs incurred within those limits. It is a formal instrument, not a suggestion.

Key Differences at a Glance
Dimension At-Risk Work LNTP
Authorization Internal management decision Written government authorization
Cost Exposure 100% contractor risk Government-funded up to ceiling
Scope Contractor-defined; discretionary Government-defined; bounded
Cost Ceiling Internal budget only Not-to-exceed (NTE) amount specified
FAR Applicability Indirect; costs must still be CAS-compliant if later billed Direct; FAR cost principles apply immediately
Revenue Recognition No revenue until/unless contract negotiated Revenue recognizable against LNTP performance
Typical Duration Weeks to months 30–120 days (usually specified)
Risk Alert

At-risk work that is later deemed unallowable under FAR 31.205 cannot be recovered through the contract—even if the government later agrees to reimburse. Every dollar of at-risk spend should be accumulated as if it will be audited by DCAA, because it might be.

Section 02

Work Authorization & Governance

Starting work before a contract is signed isn't just a financial decision. It's a governance decision. The authorization framework must be airtight—because if things go sideways, the paper trail is the only thing between your CFO and a very bad day.

Internal Authorization Framework

Regardless of whether the work is at-risk or LNTP, the contractor needs a formal internal approval process. This is not optional. It is the single most important control in the pre-award lifecycle.

  1. Business Case Preparation The Program Manager documents the strategic rationale, scope of pre-award work, estimated cost, risk mitigation plan, and the probability and timing of contract award. This isn't a one-pager—it's a decision document.
  2. Financial Impact Assessment Finance models the cash flow impact, cost recoverability scenarios (full recovery, partial, zero), and the effect on program margin, EAC, and indirect rate absorption. For LNTP, this includes validating the NTE ceiling against the planned scope.
  3. Multi-Level Approval At-risk work requires escalated approval—typically VP/GM level for amounts under a defined threshold (e.g., $500K), and executive/CEO approval above it. LNTP work follows a similar path but with legal/contracts review of the LNTP instrument itself.
  4. Work Authorization Document (WAD) Issuance A formal WAD is issued, specifying: authorized scope, budget ceiling, charge numbers, period of performance, and stop-work triggers. This becomes the control document for all subsequent cost accumulation.
  5. Stop-Work Criteria Definition Pre-defined triggers that automatically halt pre-award work: contract award delayed beyond X date, cost burn exceeds Y% of ceiling, competitive loss confirmed, or customer communicates de-scope. These must be in the WAD, not in someone's head.

LNTP-Specific Governance

When an LNTP is issued, the contractor's Contracts team must validate several additional elements before work begins:

LNTP Validation Checklist

Scope alignment — Does the LNTP scope match the proposal scope, or is it a subset? Any delta must be documented and priced.

NTE ceiling adequacy — Is the authorized ceiling sufficient to cover the planned burn rate for the LNTP period? If not, the PM must re-scope or seek ceiling increases.

Terms flow-down — Do the LNTP terms reference the anticipated contract terms (FAR/DFARS clauses, IP rights, data rights)? Gaps here create exposure at definitization.

Period of performance — Is the LNTP PoP realistic given the expected definitization timeline? Build in buffer.

Section 03

Budgeting & Cost Structure

The budgeting approach for pre-award work is where most contractors get into trouble. They treat it as "temporary" and set up lightweight tracking. Then the contract definitizes, and they spend weeks reconciling a mess. Set it up right from the start.

WBS & Charge Number Architecture

Pre-award work must be tracked in a structure that mirrors the anticipated contract WBS. This is non-negotiable for two reasons: (1) clean integration at definitization, and (2) DCAA audit readiness if the costs become billable.

Recommended WBS Structure for Pre-Award Work
Program X (Top-Level Project)
├── X.PRE — Pre-Award / At-Risk Phase  Pre-Award
├── X.PRE.ENG — Engineering / Design
├── X.PRE.PROC — Long-Lead Procurement
├── X.PRE.PM — Program Management
└── X.PRE.SUPPORT — Tooling / Test Setup
├── X.01 — Contract Phase 1 (Post-Definitization)
├── X.01.ENG
├── X.01.PROC
└── ...
└── X.02 — Contract Phase 2

The X.PRE structure allows all pre-award costs to be accumulated in dedicated WBS elements that can later be either (a) reclassified into the contract WBS at definitization, or (b) closed out and absorbed as period cost or allocated to COGS.

Budget Baseline: Two Scenarios

Scenario A: LNTP Budget

The budget baseline is the NTE ceiling from the LNTP instrument. The PM manages to this ceiling with the same rigor as a definitized contract. EVM is typically not required during LNTP, but cost/schedule tracking must be formal.

FAR 31.2 CAS 401/402

Scenario B: At-Risk Budget

The budget is internally authorized via the WAD. It represents the maximum exposure the company is willing to absorb if the contract never materializes. This is an IR&D/B&P-adjacent decision and should be governed accordingly.

FAR 31.205-18 CAS 420

Practical Guidance

Even for at-risk work, set up budgets by cost element: direct labor by skill mix, ODCs, subcontractor, travel, and material. If this work ever becomes billable, you'll need cost element detail to support the incurred cost submission. If it doesn't become billable, you'll need it for B&P pool allocation under CAS 420.

Section 04

Execution & Cost Management

Once authorized, pre-award work must be executed with the same cost discipline as a definitized contract. Actually, more discipline—because the margin for error is thinner and the consequences of overrun are less forgiving.

Cost Accumulation Rules

  1. Dedicated Charge Numbers Only All time, material, and ODC charges go to the pre-award WBS elements. No co-mingling with other programs, no "parking" costs on indirect pools to be reclassified later. DCAA will find it.
  2. Timekeeping Discipline Employees must charge directly to the pre-award charge numbers from day one. Floor checks, timecard audits, and supervisor approvals apply the same as any direct-charge program.
  3. Weekly Cost Burn Tracking The PM reviews actual vs. budget weekly. For at-risk work, if burn rate exceeds plan by >10%, stop-work evaluation triggers. For LNTP, approaching 80% of NTE ceiling triggers a formal ceiling assessment.
  4. Material & Subcontract Commitment Control Purchase orders and subcontract commitments during pre-award carry extra risk. Cancelation clauses are mandatory. No long-lead material buys without explicit executive approval and FAR clause flow-down.
  5. Segregation of Allowable vs. Unallowable If any pre-award costs include elements that would be unallowable under FAR 31.205 (e.g., entertainment, certain legal fees, lobbying), they must be segregated immediately—not at audit time.

Section 05

Contract Integration at Definitization

This is where most organizations fumble. The contract gets signed, everyone celebrates, and then Finance spends the next month trying to figure out how to merge two cost baselines into one coherent EAC. Plan the integration before the pre-award work starts.

Integration Playbook

1. Cost Reconciliation & Validation

Reconcile all costs accumulated on the pre-award WBS. Validate every charge against the WAD scope. Remove or reclassify any costs outside the authorized scope. Produce a clean incurred cost summary by cost element, by WBS.

2. Contract WBS Mapping

Map each pre-award WBS element to its corresponding contract WBS element. This is a one-to-one or many-to-one mapping—never one-to-many. The goal is traceability: every pre-award dollar must land in exactly one contract WBS element.

3. EAC / Budget Baseline Reset

The contract's Performance Measurement Baseline (PMB) must reflect the pre-award actuals as "burned" budget. BCWP for completed pre-award work is established at definitization. The remaining budget is allocated to the forward work. This is a formal Over-Target Baseline (OTB) or reprogramming action if pre-award costs exceed the contract value allocated to that scope.

4. Schedule Integration

Pre-award activities become completed milestones in the IMS. Successor activities and dependencies are updated. Critical path recalculated. Any schedule risk created by the pre-award phase is surfaced in the program risk register.

5. Financial System Cutover

In SAP or equivalent ERP: pre-award WBS elements are statistically closed (no new charges), actual costs are settled or reallocated to contract WBS elements, and the contract project is activated with full budget load. Cost element detail, commitment items, and fund assignments transfer cleanly.

SAP-Specific Note

In SAP S/4HANA, this integration typically involves settlement rules on the pre-award WBS to the definitized contract WBS, ensuring ACDOCA (Universal Journal) entries reflect the reclassification. Use activity type settlement for labor and order/WBS settlement for non-labor. Ensure cost element groups are consistent across both structures to avoid reconciliation breaks in CO-PA or Margin Analysis.

At definitization, the at-risk costs face one of three outcomes. The financial treatment differs significantly for each, and the accounting must be determined before the books close for the period in which definitization occurs.

Outcome A: At-Risk Costs Become Billable

This is the best-case scenario. During contract negotiations, the contractor successfully argues that the pre-award work was performed at the government's implicit or explicit direction and should be included in the contract value. Practically, this means:

  • Pre-award costs are reclassified from the pre-award WBS to the contract WBS
  • The contract value includes reimbursement for these costs (cost-type) or they're factored into the FFP price
  • Revenue is recognized under ASC 606 as part of the overall performance obligation
  • The EAC reflects these costs as incurred, with corresponding budget allocation

Outcome B: At-Risk Costs Absorbed into Contract

More common. The government won't pay for the at-risk work separately, but the contract is awarded. The contractor absorbs the at-risk costs as part of their cost of performance. The accounting treatment:

Under ASC 606, the at-risk costs become part of the total estimated cost at completion. They reduce the margin on the contract but are recognized proportionally as the performance obligation is satisfied over time (cost-to-cost input method). The critical point: these costs were incurred before the contract, but they contribute to satisfying the performance obligation and therefore must be included in the EAC used for percentage-of-completion calculations.

Outcome C: No Contract Awarded

The worst case. No contract, no recovery. The at-risk costs are expensed in the period and allocated to the B&P indirect cost pool per the contractor's CAS 420 disclosure statement. This increases the B&P rate, which flows to all government contracts through indirect rate allocation. It's a double hit: the direct cost is lost, and the indirect rates go up.

Section 07

LNTP Integration: A Cleaner Path

LNTP integration is mechanically simpler than at-risk because the costs are already government-authorized and the accounting treatment is established from day one. But "simpler" doesn't mean "automatic."

  1. Validate LNTP Scope → Contract Scope Alignment Confirm that every LNTP deliverable maps to a contract CLIN or SLIN. Any LNTP work that falls outside the definitized contract scope becomes a negotiation item—and potentially unrecoverable.
  2. Cost Settlement LNTP costs settle into the contract WBS at their actual incurred value. No markup, no adjustment—unless the LNTP instrument specifies fee accrual, which is rare pre-definitization.
  3. Fee / Margin Calculation Fee on LNTP costs is typically calculated and applied at definitization. The contract price includes the LNTP costs plus applicable fee, established during the definitization negotiation. The contractor's margin on LNTP work is determined retroactively.
  4. Billing Transition If the LNTP authorized interim billing (common on cost-type instruments), reconcile all LNTP billings against the definitized contract. Adjust vouchers as needed. If no interim billing was authorized, the LNTP costs become part of the first billing cycle post-definitization.

Integration Architecture

The cleanest approach is to create the pre-award project as a child of the anticipated contract project in the WBS hierarchy. At definitization, the pre-award WBS elements are statistically closed and their accumulated costs settled upward or laterally into the contract WBS. This preserves the audit trail, maintains ACDOCA integrity, and avoids the need for manual journal entries to move costs between unrelated cost objects.

Section 09

Revenue Recognition Under ASC 606

Revenue recognition for pre-award work intersects with ASC 606 in ways that require careful judgment. The core question: when does a performance obligation exist, and how do pre-award costs relate to it?

LNTP Revenue Recognition

An LNTP creates a performance obligation from the date of issuance. Revenue is recognized over time using the cost-to-cost input method, where the measure of progress is costs incurred relative to total estimated costs for the LNTP scope. At definitization, the LNTP performance obligation merges into the broader contract performance obligation, and the cumulative catch-up adjustment (if any) is recorded.

At-Risk Revenue Recognition

At-risk work creates no performance obligation until the contract is awarded. No revenue is recognized during the at-risk period. Costs are capitalized as WIP if recovery is probable (i.e., the contract is expected to be awarded), or expensed immediately if recovery is not probable.

At definitization, the accounting treatment depends on the outcome:

ASC 606 Treatment by Outcome
Outcome Revenue Impact Cost Treatment Margin Impact
Costs become billable Included in transaction price; recognized over time via cost-to-cost Reclassified to contract WBS; part of EAC Neutral (if priced at cost + fee)
Costs absorbed (non-billable) No incremental revenue; total transaction price unchanged Added to EAC; increases cost-to-complete Negative — margin compression
No contract awarded No revenue recognized Expensed to B&P or period cost Direct P&L hit + indirect rate increase
Audit Sensitivity

The decision to capitalize at-risk costs as WIP (versus expensing immediately) requires a probability assessment that must be documented. Auditors will scrutinize the basis for the contractor's conclusion that contract award was "probable." Contemporaneous documentation of the probability assessment—including the competitive landscape, customer communications, and proposal status—is essential. Retroactive justification does not pass muster.

Section 10

Risk Mitigation & Best Practices

Pre-award work is inherently risky. The contractors who do it well share common disciplines.

  1. Treat Pre-Award Like a Contract WAD, budget, charge numbers, cost reviews, stop-work triggers. If you wouldn't run a $5M contract without these controls, don't run $500K of at-risk work without them either.
  2. Document Everything in Real Time Customer direction (even informal), internal approvals, scope changes, cost status. If DCAA or your external auditor asks "why did you incur this cost?", the answer needs to exist in writing, dated before the cost was incurred.
  3. Build the Contract WBS First Design the pre-award WBS as a subset of the anticipated contract WBS. Integration at definitization should be a configuration change, not a data migration.
  4. Separate At-Risk from LNTP Costs Even if both are happening concurrently on the same program, they have different authorization levels, different cost recovery profiles, and different audit treatment. Never co-mingle.
  5. Set Hard Limits and Honor Them The WAD ceiling is not aspirational. When you hit 90% of the at-risk budget, you stop and re-evaluate. The PM who says "just one more week" is the PM who blows the budget by 40%.
  6. Negotiate Retroactive Billing Early Don't wait until definitization to raise the question of at-risk cost recovery. Build it into your negotiation strategy from the proposal phase. The earlier the customer acknowledges the at-risk investment, the stronger your position at the negotiating table.
  7. Maintain CAS Compliance Throughout Pre-award costs must be accumulated consistent with the contractor's disclosed and established cost accounting practices. A CAS noncompliance finding related to pre-award cost treatment can trigger cost impact assessments across the contractor's entire portfolio.
Conclusion

The Bottom Line

Pre-award work isn't going away. The government's acquisition timelines will continue to lag behind mission needs, and contractors will continue to start work before the ink is dry. That's the reality of defense and government contracting.

The question isn't whether you'll do at-risk or LNTP work. It's whether you'll do it with the financial discipline, governance rigor, and system architecture to protect your company when the contract definitizes—or when it doesn't.

Set up the controls. Build the WBS right. Track the costs like they matter. Because they do.
